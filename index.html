PhD Fellow at Strategic Organization Design + Digital Democracy Centre at the University of Southern Denmark. I am a sociologt and obtained my degree from the University of Copenhagen. I have now embraced my interdisciplnary tendencies and made the leap into the fields of strategy and management.

With my PhD project I have embraced my interdisciplinary tendencies and made the leap into the field of strategy and management. Drawing on perspectives from multiple fields, I investigate how coopetition increased in the Danish news media ecosystem following digitalization. I conduct citation network analysis, similarity detection between news articles and interviews with journalists and managers.

My interests concern mainly sociological notions of organizations, platforms & ecosystems, status & power, technology and social movements.

selected publications

  1. MSc thesis
    Nonhuman actants on the frontlines of #Hongkong: a digital sociological investigation of the Anti-ELAB movement’s symbol construction on Twitter
    E. B. Johansen
    Unpublished article in Danish Jun 2021
    Abs PDF

    During 2019, several demonstrations took place in the streets of Hong Kong, where protesters collided with police forces due to unsatisfaction with a proposed extradition law that would allow for Hong Kong citizens to be extradited to mainland China for prosecution. The Anti-ELAB movement behind the protests became known for their innovative mobilization of objects and technologies in its resistance to police forces and outmaneuvering of surveillance cameras. Umbrellas were transformed into shields to avoid the blasts of watercannons, laser beams blinded surveillance cameras to obfuscate their gazes, and masks were worn to diminish the suffocating effects of teargas and hide the identity of protesters. These findings were the result of a prior study I co-authored on the Hong Kong 2019 protests that focused on the mobilization of objects at the street level (Johansen, Mariager & Nissen 2019). However, these objects were also made present on social media platforms such as Twitter. Here, the objects did not combat police forces and surveillance efforts directly, but instead served as symbolic and visual representatives for the movement. Thus, certain objects and technologies became central both as practical tools and visual symbols in the Anti-ELAB movement. To illuminate the above, this paper examines how practical objects and technologies over time became the foundation of the movement’s symbol construction process, and how the resulting symbols are dynamic and temporally conditioned. To illuminate this, I utilize a quali-quant based research design and digital methods such as dictionary classification, deep learning object detection and temporal network analysis to process and analyze 1,6 million tweets about the Hong Kong protests during 2019. Furthermore, I draw upon actor-network theory and see non-human objects and technologies as actors in the same vein as humans. Doing so, I show both the prevalence of three non-human actors that proved to be central to the movement (umbrellas, lasers, and masks) and the different translations they underwent over time in the tweets. With this temporal approach, I illustrate how the non-human actors oscillate between practical-confrontative translations, where the actors’ functions are primarily to sabotage the aims of authorities and surveillance, and symbolic-visual translations in which the primary function becomes visualizing the presence of the movement in symbolic and peaceful ways. Through this analysis, I uncover the Anti-ELAB movement’s process of symbol construction and show how it is characterized by high acceleration and deceleration, causing some non-human actors to become only momentary symbols. I conclude by suggesting the need for a more dynamic and processual approach to studying symbols within the literature on social movements in the digital age. However, more studies using the analytical approach I present with this paper are needed, to corroborate this claim.

  2. SocArXiv
    Revolution of Our Time: Surveillance and Materialiy in the 2019 Hong Kong Protests
    E. B. Johansen, J. E. Mariager, and V. K. Nissen
    SocArXiv Papers May 2021
    Abs PDF

    On February 12, 2019, the Hong Kong government proposed a legal change that would allow transfer of certain criminal suspects to mainland China. This proposition ignited demonstrations initially against the proposition itself, and later against the Hong Kong government and Chief Executive, Carrie Lam. Since then, millions of protesters have been demonstrating for the political independence of Hong Kong. During these demonstrations, the use of objects and technologies by protesters in their confrontation with local authorities and surveillance devices have been especially highlighted by news media. Through an actor-network analysis, we examine how the presence of surveillance influences the central human and non-human actors of the protest. We deploy a theoretical and methodological approach based on a synthesis between actor-network theory and visual sociology. Our empirical foundation for this analysis is 83 photographs that serve as second-hand insight into the protest situation. Through this qualitative analysis, we illuminate the role of non-human actors in the Hong Kong protests. Also, how the context of protesting in a surveillance state influences the way the networks of the protests are organized. The analytical results point to relations of surveillance and counter-surveillance strategies as central connections between actors in the protest networks. We conclude that non-human actors played a central role for the movement and were mobilized in both practical and visual ways, the latter resulting in non-human actors serving as organizational visual markers.
